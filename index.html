About Felix Morelo

Felix Morelo, a Colombian-American artist, has become a prominent figure in the New York City art scene with his distinctive and engaging street art. Known for his whimsical chalk drawings, Morelo has transformed the mundane sidewalks of NYC into interactive canvases that invite public participation and provoke thought. His work, which includes Good Luck and Bad Luck Spots, has been featured in multiple media outlets, including The New York Times, where Amelia Nierenberg writes:

“Felix Morelo’s chalk circles give parks, paths, and pavements a burst of spiritual energy.”

Early Life and Inspiration

Born in the United States, Morelo grew up with deep ties to his Colombian heritage. He moved to New York City at the age of eleven. His passion for art was evident from an early age, and he pursued formal education in the arts, studying painting at Parsons School of Design and drawing at The Art Students League of New York City. Initially, Morelo’s work consisted primarily of chalk-drawn faces scattered throughout the city. However, his artistic journey took a pivotal turn in 2010 when he began to create his now-famous “Good Luck Spots”.

The Good Luck and Bad Luck Spots

The concept of the "Good Luck Spots" emerged as an evolution of Morelo's earlier work. Encouraged by a fellow artist to provide a conclusive element to his trails of chalk faces, Morelo introduced these spots as whimsical, interactive installations. Each spot, marked by a chalk circle, features messages such as “Good Luck Spot,” “Bad Luck Spot,” “Hugging Spot,” “Screaming Spot,” “Laughing Spot,” and more. These installations are not just artistic expressions but social experiments aimed at challenging superstitions, taboos, and everyday routines.

His controversial spots like “Racist,” “Suicide,” and “Israel and Palestine” have sparked street attacks, death threats, and intense debates among strangers, reflecting the power of his art to incite discussion and reflection. Morelo’s spots can be found in various parks and streets across Manhattan, including Union Square and Washington Square Park. The intention behind these spots is to engage passersby, inviting them to interact with the artwork and, by extension, with each other. This interactive element is a core aspect of Morelo's work, encouraging viewers to break out of their daily routines and engage in spontaneous social interactions.

Artistic Philosophy and Impact

Felix Morelo's work is deeply rooted in the exploration of human psychology and social behavior. His chalk circles, often brightly colored and whimsically designed, serve as more than just art; they are tools for social interaction and community building. By drawing people into these spots, Morelo challenges them to question their beliefs and engage with their surroundings in new ways. This approach is particularly resonant in the post-pandemic world, where social distancing and isolation have become common themes. Morelo’s spots offer a playful yet poignant reminder of the importance of human connection.

In addition to his SPOT drawings, Morelo's portfolio further explores human duality through his “CUBES” series. This series presents order and stability where organic and chaotic experiences, feelings, desires, dreams, nightmares, wishes, and fantasies are housed within boxes or cubes. In both series, memories can be played out, explored, and analyzed; one immediate, the other introspective.

Exhibitions and Public Recognition

While Morelo’s chalk drawings are his most recognizable work, he also creates murals and portable art pieces. His work has been featured at various venues such as Lehman College, New Jersey City University - Lemmerman Gallery, The Blackburn 20/20 Gallery under Robert Blackburn Printmaking Workshop, Newfields – Indianapolis Museum of Art, The Raynham Hall Museum of Oyster Bay, NYC Galleries including Howl Happening and Time to Be Happy, and international shows at Fondazione Giorgio Cini in Venice, Italy, and Dianxi Science and Technology Normal University in Lingang, China. His work has garnered both local and international attention, emphasizing the cultural and social impact of his art. His innovative approach to public art has earned him a place among New York City's most beloved street artists.

The New York City Artist Corps awarded him a grant, and five commissioned murals reach audiences daily in New York City and Mexico. He is represented by Artist Abbey Gallery in Truth or Consequences, New Mexico, and Mique Gallery in Tokyo, Japan.

Entrepreneurial Ventures

In addition to his public installations, Morelo runs a mini portable storefront, where he sells painted paper versions of his spots. This entrepreneurial aspect of his practice allows him to share his art with a broader audience, making his whimsical creations accessible to anyone who wishes to take a piece of his street art home. Morelo also dabbles in performance art, poetry, sculpting, printmaking, and bookmaking, showcasing his versatility as an artist and his deep engagement with the New York City art community.

Conclusion

Felix Morelo’s art transcends simple visual aesthetics; it is a dynamic and interactive experience that fosters community and challenges social norms. Through his chalk circles and other creative endeavors, Morelo has made a lasting impact on the cultural landscape of New York City. His work continues to inspire, provoke, and bring joy to the countless individuals who encounter it daily. As Morelo himself often reflects, his art is about breaking barriers, fostering connections, and inviting everyone to find a little magic in the everyday.
